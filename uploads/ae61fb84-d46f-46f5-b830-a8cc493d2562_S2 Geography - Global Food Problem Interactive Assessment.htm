SECTION B - Question 1: Challenges to Food Production (6 marks)
Model Answer:
1. Overuse of freshwater resources: Agriculture consumes 70% of freshwater. Overuse leads to water scarcity, limiting irrigation for crops and reducing agricultural productivity.
2. Land degradation: Soil erosion, desertification, and nutrient depletion reduce land fertility, decreasing crop yields and limiting areas suitable for farming.
3. Extreme weather events: Climate change causes droughts, floods, and unpredictable weather patterns that destroy crops, disrupt planting schedules, and reduce harvests.
SECTION B - Question 2: Farming System (8 marks)
Model Answer:
a) Inputs: Labor, Capital (or Physical resources, Seeds, Water, Fertilizers) - 2 marks
b) Processes: Sowing, Harvesting (or Irrigating, Feeding, Cultivating) - 2 marks
c) Outputs: Food crops, Raw materials (or Vegetables, Livestock products) - 2 marks
d) Explanation: Farming is a system because it involves interconnected components where inputs (resources) are transformed through processes (farming activities) to produce outputs (products). Each component affects others, forming a complete cycle. - 2 marks
SECTION B - Question 3: Classification of Farming (6 marks)
Model Answer:
1. By intensity: Intensive farming vs. Extensive farming (Example: Rice paddies in Asia use high inputs on small areas = intensive; Cattle ranching in Australia uses low inputs on large areas = extensive) - 2 marks
2. By output type: Arable (crops), Pastoral (livestock), Mixed (both) (Example: Wheat farming = arable; Sheep farming = pastoral; Farm with crops and animals = mixed) - 2 marks
3. By purpose: Subsistence vs. Commercial (Example: Family farm growing rice for own consumption = subsistence; Large farm producing wheat for sale = commercial) - 2 marks
SECTION B - Question 4: Hong Kong's Limited Active Farmland (5 marks)
Model Answer:
1. Urban development pressure: Hong Kong has limited land (1,104 km¬≤ total). Urban areas, housing, and infrastructure development are prioritized over farming due to higher economic returns and population density, leaving only 4.5% for agriculture. - 2.5 marks
2. Low profitability and labor shortage: Farming in Hong Kong generates low income compared to other industries. High operating costs, competition from cheap imports from Mainland China, and lack of young farmers willing to continue agricultural work result in farmland abandonment. - 2.5 marks
SECTION B - Question 5: Innovative Farming Methods (5 marks)
Model Answer:
1. Hydroponics: Growing plants in nutrient-rich water without soil. This method is necessary because Hong Kong has extremely limited farmland (only 50 km¬≤, with 15% actively used). Hydroponics maximizes production in small spaces, uses less water, and allows year-round cultivation independent of weather. - 2.5 marks
2. Rooftop farming: Utilizing building rooftops for agriculture. This is necessary to make use of unused urban spaces in Hong Kong's dense urban environment, bringing food production closer to consumers, reducing transportation costs, and increasing local food supply without requiring additional land. - 2.5 marks

SECTION C - Question 2b: Food Imports (4 marks)
Model Answer:
Hong Kong relies heavily on food imports from Mainland China for several reasons: First, Hong Kong has very limited agricultural land (only 50 km¬≤ or 4.5% of total land area), with only 15% actively farmed, making local production insufficient to meet the needs of 7.5 million people. Second, local farming faces high operating costs and cannot compete with cheaper imports from Mainland China. Third, geographical proximity to Mainland China makes transportation convenient and cost-effective. Fourth, historical and economic ties facilitate smooth trade relationships and supply chains. As a result, Hong Kong can only produce a small fraction of its food needs and must import most food items.
SECTION C - Question 2c: Farming Comparison (8 marks)
Model Answer:
Traditional Farming: Requires large land areas with soil cultivation, depends heavily on weather conditions, has seasonal limitations, uses more water and natural resources, typically produces lower yields per unit area, and is labor-intensive. In Hong Kong's context, traditional farming is increasingly difficult due to land scarcity and urban development pressure.

Innovative Methods (Hydroponics/Rooftop Farming):
Advantages:
1. Space efficiency: Can be implemented in urban areas, on rooftops, or in vertical structures, making maximum use of limited space in Hong Kong's dense urban environment.
2. Controlled environment: Protected from extreme weather, pests, and diseases, ensuring more stable and reliable production year-round.
3. Higher yields: Optimized growing conditions lead to faster growth cycles and greater productivity per square meter compared to traditional farming.
4. Water conservation: Hydroponics uses up to 90% less water than traditional farming as water is recirculated in closed systems.
5. Reduced pesticide use: Controlled environments minimize pest problems, reducing need for chemical pesticides and producing safer food.
6. Urban food security: Brings food production closer to consumers, reducing transportation costs and carbon footprint while increasing local food supply.
7. Year-round production: Independent of seasons, ensuring consistent food availability throughout the year.
